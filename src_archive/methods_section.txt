A coupled surface–leaf–soil biophysical model was used to evaluate urban tree heat stress under alternative surface material scenarios. The model simulates hourly leaf temperature, transpiration, and stress metrics by integrating: (i) 3D radiative transfer (Radiance), (ii) ground surface energy balance, (iii) canopy energy balance (Li et al., 2023), (iv) root-zone soil moisture dynamics, and (v) species-specific physiological response functions. The workflow supports scenario comparison for both landscape (ground) and façade materials by propagating material changes through the radiative environment and biophysical calculations.

2.1 Digital site model and tree–sensor mapping
A 3D digital site model was assembled to represent building volumes, façade surfaces, and ground materials, together with mapped vegetation locations. Opaque surfaces were parameterized with material properties required for radiative and thermal calculations, emphasizing shortwave albedo \(\alpha\) and longwave emissivity \(\varepsilon\), and materials were grouped as landscape (horizontal ground) or façade (vertical surfaces). A sensor point grid was generated in the model domain and used as the interface between 3D radiative transfer and tree-scale biophysics. Each tree \(i\) at \((x_i,y_i,z_i)\) was assigned the nearest sensor \(j\) in 3D Euclidean space:

\[
d_{ij}=\sqrt{(x_i-x_j)^2+(y_i-y_j)^2+(z_i-z_j)^2}, \qquad j^*=\arg\min_j d_{ij}.
\]

Hourly irradiance components simulated at sensor \(j^*\) were then attributed to tree \(i\) and used to drive the subsequent ground, surface, and leaf energy balance calculations. This nearest-sensor coupling preserves spatial heterogeneity from buildings and surface materials while remaining computationally tractable for many trees and scenarios. Species-specific parameters (e.g., sky-view factor, leaf radiative properties, stomatal response parameters) were applied consistently across scenarios.

2.2 Radiative transfer and irradiance components
Hourly irradiance fields were simulated in Radiance using a two-phase, direct–diffuse workflow to resolve the radiative environment at each sensor point. Here, \(K\) denotes shortwave (solar) irradiance (W m\(^{-2}\)), with \(K_{\downarrow}\) the downwelling component at the sensor (direct + diffuse from the sky hemisphere) and \(K_{\uparrow}\) the upwelling component (shortwave reflected from surrounding surfaces). The output forcing at each sensor \(i\) was partitioned into (i) downwelling direct-beam irradiance, (ii) downwelling diffuse irradiance, and (iii) upwelling reflected irradiance:

\[
K_{\downarrow}(t)=K_{\downarrow,\mathrm{dir}}(t)+K_{\downarrow,\mathrm{dif}}(t), \qquad K_{\uparrow}(t)=K_{\uparrow,\mathrm{dir}}(t)+K_{\uparrow,\mathrm{dif}}(t).
\]

Upwelling shortwave at a sensor was approximated post-raytracing using local material albedo: \(K_{\uparrow}(i,t)=\alpha_i\,K_{\downarrow}(i,t)\). Longwave radiation was calculated from atmospheric conditions and surface temperatures rather than explicit raytracing. Downwelling longwave from the sky (\(L_{\downarrow}\)) was computed from air temperature and relative humidity using standard atmospheric emissivity relationships. Upwelling longwave from surfaces (\(L_{\uparrow}\)) was calculated as \(L_{\uparrow}=\varepsilon\,\sigma\,T_{\mathrm{surf}}^4\), where surface temperature was determined from the ground energy balance (Section 2.4). All scenarios used identical geometry, sensor points, and Radiance settings, so differences in radiation forcing reflect only changes in material properties (\(\alpha\), \(\varepsilon\)). These shortwave and longwave components were then used to compute net radiation in the ground and leaf energy balances.

2.3 Material scenario construction
Material scenarios were constructed to isolate the influence of urban surface properties on tree radiative forcing and physiological stress. Surface materials were assigned to landscape and façade elements via grid identifiers carried through the Radiance pipeline, enabling consistent mapping between simulated irradiance outputs and the underlying surfaces. Each material was defined by shortwave albedo \(\alpha\) and longwave emissivity \(\varepsilon\) (and, where needed for ground temperature calculations, effective thermal properties including heat capacity and evaporation factor). Alternative scenarios were generated by swapping material assignments for selected landscape and/or façade elements while holding all other inputs constant (geometry, tree locations, sensor grid, and weather). Each scenario was then re-simulated in Radiance using the same direct–diffuse workflow to produce comparable hourly irradiance components at each sensor.

Scenario effects enter the energy balances primarily through shortwave absorption and longwave exchange. For example, the net shortwave at a surface can be expressed as:

\[
K_{\mathrm{net}}(t)=(1-\alpha)\,K_{\downarrow}(t)-K_{\uparrow}(t),
\]

so changing \(\alpha\) modifies both local absorption and (via \(K_{\uparrow}\)) the reflected field experienced by nearby trees. Scenario outputs were stored as sensor-grid time series and mapped to trees (Section 2.1) for biophysical evaluation.

2.4 Biophysical simulation period and risk metrics
Tree stress was evaluated for the warmest week of the year (168 h), identified using a 7-day running mean of dry-bulb air temperature. For each tree and scenario, ground temperature \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\) was estimated hourly using a one-layer surface energy balance accounting for thermal mass, absorbed shortwave (modulated by albedo), longwave exchange (modulated by emissivity), and sensible and latent heat fluxes. Material-specific heat capacity and evaporation factors (0 for impervious, 0.3–0.8 for pervious/vegetated surfaces) were assigned based on surface type. Leaf temperature \(T_{\mathrm{leaf}}\) was then computed using the steady-state Canopy Energy Balance (CEB) model of Li et al. (2023), which receives scenario-specific shortwave irradiance and ground temperature as inputs. Material albedo affects leaf temperature through reflected shortwave, while emissivity affects it through ground cooling and upwelling longwave radiation.

Stomatal resistance was dynamically calculated from soil moisture stress: root-zone soil moisture \(\theta\) was updated hourly using a one-dimensional water balance driven by precipitation, evapotranspiration, and drainage. Soil moisture stress scaled stomatal resistance, creating a feedback where dry conditions reduce transpiration and elevate leaf temperature. Risk was summarized using threshold exceedance hours for heat stress (\(T_{\mathrm{leaf}} > T_{\mathrm{crit}}\)), VPD stress (\(\mathrm{VPD} > \mathrm{VPD}_{\mathrm{thr}}\)), and drought stress (\(\theta < \theta_{\mathrm{crit}}\)), combined into a weighted risk index (WRI) and evaluated as stress reduction relative to a baseline.
